A short story

Mary Brown and the Normalisation of Deviance

by Oz P.

Where to start? I'm Mary Brown. I turned eighty-six in the spring. My husband George died two years ago and I daresay he'd been keeping me honest. Funny thing is, I don't think I realised quite how much until after he was gone.

I was on my way to see him for our usual catch-up, when this woman got on the bus with one of those wee yappy dogs. The ugly thing started growling at me and I got all flustered. The lassie was pulling at the lead and telling me not to worry but I moved seats anyway. It wasn’t until I was off the bus and halfway to the graveyard that I realised I’d left my George’s flowers behind. I’m not one for making a fuss, but I always take flowers for him. Always. I was annoyed with myself, stupid old woman. It's a short walk from the bus stop, and on the way, I passed one of those memorials by the side of the road. You know what I mean: ribbons, candles, posters and cards covering this lamppost, all dedicated to a lad that had died on his moped, the poor soul. I had a wee look at it all. Framed photographs of him, messages from loved ones. There were even some flowers. Plenty of them too, so it's not like anyone would have missed one bunch. I was stealing from a lamppost, if anything. So I took them. My George wouldn’t have liked that. He'd probably have told me to leave them where they were, so I never told him where they'd come from and he never asked.

I’ve never stolen anything of consequence. I’m sure I dabbled with the usual, mind. A few penny sweets when I was a wee bairn, some soap from a hotel in Oban, a five-pound note I found outside the butcher’s and kept for myself. Everyone has examples like that; haven’t you? I’m not even sure the story with the money counts, anyway. The butcher would probably have pocketed it himself, had I handed it in. When I was a wee girl, finding money on the street was considered good luck. And you know what they say about luck, don’t you? Some days you're the pigeon; some days you're the statue.

Lord knows I’ve served my time as the statue. Everyone in my family has, really. I was raised by my grandparents, lovely people they were. They stepped in when my mother disappeared, after the rumour mill got started. People can be vicious, and in those days what folk thought of you was everything. My grandparents taught me to be strong in the face of adversity. You do what you have to survive, don't you?

Listen to me wittering on! What was I saying? I was telling you about visiting my George, wasn’t I? Let me tell you how I met him. I was at a dance hall in town with my friend Betty. One of her acquaintances introduced herself to me. She was there with George, but I don’t think it was anything serious. He shook hands with me and Betty, and was the perfect gentleman. Betty’s friend mentioned he worked for McEwan’s and that’s all I remember from that night. Well, wouldn’t you know it, a couple of weeks later I found myself in the Fountainbridge area and bumped into him by the workers’ entrance. It’s a small world and it was even smaller back then. From that point it was me on his arm in the dance hall.

We got married in 1962 and moved in together within walking distance of McEwan’s. George was the breadwinner, but I was raised not to rest on my laurels, so I contributed too. Just a few hours in the local library, but every little helped. Of course, I kept the house prim and proper and he always came home to a hot dinner. Back then we still thought we could have children, so we were waiting for that to happen. When you’re young you think you have all the time in the world, so we weren’t worried. People didn't travel as much back then, so it wasn't unusual to stay put, with the odd trip to the seaside for your holidays. Later we’d dream of more exotic locations, but we always planned to travel as a family. Our honeymoon was in North Berwick, and afterwards George and I would often go back and spend our time in the tearooms and walking along the seafront. On the sunnier days we’d walk on the sand. George would roll his trousers up, tuck his socks into his pocket and carry his shoes. I used to tease him for brushing every single grain of sand from his feet before his socks and shoes went back on. We had a favourite tearoom that we always used to visit. I’ve still got one of their spoons. I’ve always loved an afternoon tea. The crockery, the scones, the cream, the jam. The ritual of it all. I used to tell him that we should visit Scone. Move there, even. With a name like that, I bet they have the best tearooms. We could have toured them all, I would have loved that.

Anyway, where was I? The sixties, I think? It was a blur, really. We were so happy together. We were always out dancing. George got involved in the snooker club and I’d watch him play. He was already on the social club committee, putting on raffles, dances and trips. Those trips helped us travel beyond North Berwick and over the years we visited Pitlochry, Oban and St Andrews. We saved up and moved out to a bigger house, hoping that we could start a family to fill it. We spent the rest of our lives in this house, but it was always just us. Now it’s just me.

We never struggled, but we never seemed to get anywhere either. Next year was always on the horizon and we always looked forward to it. In my thirties people stopped asking us about children, and I suppose we stopped hoping. After that, George hurt his back at work, and I had to increase my hours at the library. It was only going to be for a while until they could find something for George in the office. That never happened though, and we made do as best we could.

After the accident, George’s snooker cue went into the loft and his dancing shoes to the back of the cupboard. Neither saw the light of day again. I used to say that he’d swapped the baize for books and the trot for a television. I’d bring him home books all the time; one of the perks of the job. He liked adventure novels, but I'd sneak all sorts into the pile, and he'd read every last one.

Thank God for libraries, eh? Yes, it paid the bills, but it was always a lot more than that to me. I’ve always loved my books; fiction and non-fiction. When I was younger it was all Catherine Cookson and Agatha Christie, but once I’d read all of those, I found myself buried in a Bill Bryson, reading about his travels. I was born and raised in Edinburgh, and I'd always stayed put, here or hereabouts. Maybe that's why I enjoyed reading about other places. France. America. Australia. I always wanted to travel, but we never had the money for it. After George’s injury, he wasn’t up to it. By the time he got sick, there was no chance of us going anywhere. The final nail in the coffin, if you’ll pardon the expression. Perth would have been nice. Not Perthshire, mind, I mean Perth in Australia. I always fancied that, for some reason.

It’s some world we live in, by the way. I’ve read about so many countries that I’ve never set foot in. Not just the places, but the history and politics too. Allan Pease was another one I liked, but his books were all about people and why they are the way they are. They were quite popular at the time.

Anyway, the library kept us afloat and in books, but it kept me socialising, too. George found it difficult to move about, and so became well acquainted with our settee. He used to say “You go out and see your pals, don’t worry about me. Someone has to guard the tele.” I could tell he felt he was a burden, but he never was to me.

It was at work I met Pat Ferguson. Brenda Scott came later, and there were a few others over the years. Not at the same time, or always on the same shifts, of course. It’s a good job I had them, as our pals from McEwan’s stopped calling for us after George stopped working. Pat and I were good friends, to a point. We worked together but socialised too. There was a bit of an age gap, but she didn’t seem to mind spending time with an old fogey like me. She would often turn up at work with an old biscuit tin full of homemade biscuits, scones, and the odd cake. They were good for passing the time with a cuppa, not so good for my waistline! She was artistic too. Every now and then we’d spend an afternoon making bunting and decorations for whatever display was coming up next. I was just doing the cutting and sticking mind; I’d leave the tricky bits up to her. You’d never have guessed she was younger than me; she was the one that taught me how to crochet. Not that I was ever any good at it.

We became friendly with the regulars too. I’d get to know their names from their library cards, and we’d often have a wee chat, but I’d fill in the gaps to pass the time. There was a Miss Hanley, I think it was, who read Mills & Boon at a phenomenal rate. That woman loved a good bodice ripper. She didn’t wear a wedding ring but had a heart-shaped pendant that she wore every day. I always wondered whether she’d lost her love prematurely. I used to tell Pat she'd never find a man who could live up to the ones in those books. There were others, I’m trying to think of who else… oh, I’ve forgotten his name but there was this wee bairn who borrowed the same book over and over, religiously. It was about trains. I said to Pat that he must want to travel. She said I was projecting; I disagreed but I let her have the last word. I told my George she was like that. He just laughed and said, "Nobody's perfect." Mind you, Pat would always bring in extra biscuits for me to take home to him, so of course he’d be on her side.

I shouldn’t say that. My George was always trying to keep me on the straight and narrow. Sometimes I’d come in from work all flustered and I’d have to get whatever it was off my chest to him. Who it was, the daft thing they’d done, and how I thought I’d put it right. He’d always remain calm and let me say my piece. He’d sit there, his hands folded together into a triangle, nodding away. Sometimes he’d raise an eyebrow, and if he did, I knew I might have gone too far. Sometimes I’d get cross because he hadn’t even been there. How could he have an opinion? When I'd said my piece, he'd suggest I put the kettle on. We'd have a cup of tea and a biscuit and he'd quietly say, "Perhaps you should leave it be, Mary." I was always a lot more receptive after a cuppa. He said “Leave it be” so many times that I even considered putting it on his gravestone. I went with ‘loving husband’ instead. It seemed more appropriate.

Pat and I were friends, but we had a little disagreement years ago. Funny how things change. We got on well for years, but somewhere along the line things started to sour. Actually, it wasn't one argument; it was a few little ones, truth be told. Before either of us knew it, we'd drifted apart. I was head librarian by then, but she was always questioning everything I did. After a while we stopped working on the displays together. I’d come back from my lunch break, and she’d have put up something she’d already made. It wasn’t just the displays. She'd always been one for doing things by the book, no pun intended, but around that time she seemed to ramp it up. Trying to fine little old ladies and wee bairns for being a day or two late with their returns. I'd say, "What difference does a day make?" And she'd tell me we shouldn't be making exceptions for some people and not others, that everyone should be treated the same. I thought it depended on the situation.

Towards the end, we were falling out over everything. She was always talking about modernising things. I told her, it’s a library, not NASA. We don’t need any of that. It’d been working fine as it was, thank you very much. The final straw was when she made comments about my George, asking when I was going to retire and keep him company. We relied on that money and that was my time out of the house. What business was it of hers? And why would she say that unless she was after my job? I thought we were better friends than that. I told her so, and she didn’t like it. She changed shifts after that, so I didn’t see much of her. I got a call one day from a library up by where she lived at the time. She must have applied for a job there. They asked me what I thought of her, so I told them. Eventually she left the library, I don’t know where she ended up. By that point Brenda had replaced her on my shift and the two of us got on just fine.

I bumped into Pat a few months back, if you can believe it. She lives around here now. Hadn’t seen her in years. All of a sudden, she’s in my local Post Office. I used to wonder what had become of her, and there she was in the queue with a face like a smacked behind. Some things never change. I’m sure she saw me, but she didn’t say hello. Jean at number twenty-eight told me why she’d suddenly shown up around here. She knew Pat through a friend of a friend and had heard she was looking to downsize, so told her about those new retirement flats by the Co-op. Mind you, even if she’d not been the one to bring Pat over this way, you can bet that she’d have been all over it. Nothing happens around here without Jean knowing about it. And more importantly, telling everyone else. That’s bound to rub some people up the wrong way. I expect that’s why someone scratched her car a while back.

That’s not the only strange thing that’s happened around here. According to Jean, there’s a woman across the road from me who bought some sort of fancy radio on the internet and left it sitting on her front step all day. Apparently, she was always doing that with parcels and, of course, in the end it got pinched. She told Jean that the company wouldn’t send a replacement, but they gave her the money back. Not long afterwards, she was walking past a charity shop on the high street and saw one just like it in the window. A big red thing. Not my style at all. She bought it, good as new and at a discount. So, no harm done there, I suppose.

It’s all over the street, really. Even Joe next door had his bird feeder taken, but between you and me I’m glad. First, it was attracting pigeons who’d mess everywhere, and then the crows turned up and started scaring off the pigeons, making a racket all the time. I know I get up early, but that’s my choice… not because some awful crow is screaming. My George would have probably said that they were just animals being animals. There’s more now I think about it. Things getting pinched, or damaged. It's been one thing after another lately.

I suppose that’s why you’re here, constable? The crime wave?

Like all that business with Liz at number ten, and those kids next door. Her neighbour Jim was away getting rehabilitation after breaking his hip. His grandson ended up moving in and making it into a non-stop party. Music all hours. Constant visitors. Folk smoking in the garden and on the pavement outside. Scaring the life out of the neighbours. Poor Liz was terrified of them, as well as being exhausted from being kept up by the music all night. She’s older than me, you know. God knows how she coped with all of that. I popped over for a cup of tea one day, and she was so tired her eyes were closing from the moment she opened the door. She ended up dropping off in the middle of our chat. Not for the first time, I said “Liz, you need to call the police.” She was terrified of the repercussions though, so I did her a wee favour and made the call whilst she was having a nap. When the police came round to see what was what, they must have realised something was off. When they got hold of Jim, he sent his son over to sort it all out. There was a locksmith there the next day and we’ve not seen the grandson since. Jean told me that last part, of course.

Surely you’re not here for a bit of mistaken identity on the telephone? I never said I was Liz; I just said I’m calling from number ten. George would have told me not to stick my beak in, but I’m sure Jim is happy to know his house is no longer Studio 54. Liz is sleeping better now, I know that much. Sometimes folk just need a helping hand. Sometimes folk need a wee shove.

Listen to me natter on! You can tell I’ve not had company here in some while, can’t you? You remind me of Jason, Joe next door’s grandson. He’s helped me in the garden, once or twice. He’s a kind young man. You must be about the same age. You know what they say about policemen getting younger! I was expecting someone older when I saw the police car pull up. Are you new in the job or are your shoes always that shiny? No need to answer, dear. You finish your biscuit. Have as many as you like. You’ll need some more tea to wash it down. Let me top you up, you’ve hardly touched that one. It must be stone cold. You could do with filling out a bit, if you don’t mind me saying. Your uniform is hanging off you! There you go, get that down you. The doily under the teapot is one of my crochet efforts, by the way. Although it looks less like a doily and more like a fishing net, doesn’t it? I told you I wasn’t very good!

I can tell that you were raised well, you’re so patient and polite. You’ve let me yap on for ages! My George always said I could talk for Scotland. He would say that, though. He was never much of a talker. We were a good match like that. I liked to talk and he liked to listen. Once I retired, I had no one else to talk to but him. There were the neighbours too, but back then it was a wave hello and that’s that. These days I have a wee chat with some, like Jean, but it’s usually just a minute or two on the street. At least at the library I could chat with colleagues and the regulars. I would have kept going, if only for that, but I retired because they closed it down. Ours was being merged with a bigger one and they didn’t need me anymore. I don’t like the new one, it’s full of plastic toys and screaming children. I went from time to time to top up my books, but I didn’t enjoy it. Nowadays I can’t be bothered going all that way.

I’m turning into George and watching tele all the time. He’d laugh at that, I bet. He’d say I was always on at him to stop watching the box. I’d say his eyes would go square. Mind you, his eyesight did go in the end, but I don’t think it was the tele. Everything seemed to give up on him in the end. He wasn’t able to do much, so I became his nurse, in a way. He had this big plastic pill dispenser. It’s in the kitchen, actually. I couldn’t bring myself to throw it away. I’ve still got all of his things. I’d get his prescriptions and organise them for him. You get to know them by sight after a while. Two wee blue ones, a white one with a diamond on it, and so on. It started with something for the pain, then something for his blood pressure, then something for his arthritis, then something to help him sleep. He was in so much pain towards the end, but he was determined to stay at home with me.

I would have given anything to go dancing with him again, or maybe another walk along the seafront, but by then those days were already long gone. I would have just settled for a glimpse of the old George, before his injury. He had this cheeky grin, and I missed that so much. In the end, I suppose my biggest hope was that he wouldn’t be in pain any longer. And then one day, he wasn’t. George got his wish and stayed here with me until the end. We’re still together, really. There’s not a day that goes by that I don’t think about him. I go and visit him regularly and tell him what’s been going on. He’s always been a good listener.

You’re a good listener too, aren’t you?

It’s nice to chat with someone. Since my George left me, I’ve been drifting along. At the start it was rough, of course. Almost a lifetime together and a big chunk of that was spent caring for him. It’s a shock when your life changes so violently. I’d be calling his name when I’d get in from the shops or talking to him from another room. I still do that to be honest; if I didn’t talk to myself, who would I talk to? You’re sat in his chair, you know. Sometimes I look over and expect George to be sitting there.

I suppose I’ve settled into a routine. I’ve always read plenty. I watch the tele. I visit the charity shops on the high street. Mind you, just lately I’m dropping things off more than buying. I’m not tight but I’ve never been one for wasting my money. “Look after the pennies and the pounds will look after themselves,” as they say. I’ve heard worse advice. I’ll buy the odd pretty trinket for my cabinet, paperweights, you know. I found a lovely mug with a painting of the harbour in North Berwick. What are the chances of that? It’ll sound daft to you, but I felt like that was George saying hello. When I found it, I was still browsing, so I carried it around the shop with me. I must have put it in my bag so I could use both hands when I was looking at the clothes. It wasn't until I got home that I realised it was in there. What was I meant to do, go all the way back? I’m always donating things to them. Good things too. Books, ornaments, gadgets. It all balances out in the end, doesn't it? My George would be laughing. He used to call that Mary’s maths.

Is that why you’re here, constable? Seems a bit much for a wee souvenir mug, even if you knew about the flowers as well. Mind you, you probably knew the rest already. About the radio and Joe’s vermin magnet, I mean. Not that either of them learnt their lessons, mind. Even now she’s leaving parcels sitting on the front step, where anybody could help themselves, and Joe still thinks he’s Dr Doolittle. If my George was here, I’m sure he’d be thinking “I told you to leave it be.”

Did someone accuse me of something? Was I caught on one of those doorbells with the cameras inside? Was it that woman from number eighteen? I wouldn’t listen to her; she’s a strange one. Waters her plants at night. There’s something off about her. I don’t know why I’m asking… you’re not going to spill the beans, are you?

Sit up straight, dear. You’re dribbling all over my cushions. And you’ve got biscuit crumbs all over your uniform.

I don’t know, first Liz drops off mid-conversation, and now you! I hope that’s not the effect I have on people these days. Can you hear me? No? Those pills are still good then. They used to knock poor George out, but you’re young and healthy, so I wasn’t sure how many it would take. You didn’t finish the pot, but you had enough to do the trick, by the look of you.

What to do now, eh? You did give me a fright by the way, turning up unannounced like that. There'll be folks wondering where you are by now, I bet.

This is a bit of a mess, isn’t it?

I expect I’ll get into trouble. It might have been a slap on the wrist for pinching the mug, but I suppose this is a bit different, isn’t it? I wonder what they do with folks my age. Will I be sent somewhere? I’d rather stay here; this is our home. I could wear one of those electronic things around my ankle, maybe. I’ve read about those in the paper.

I wouldn’t be the first in my family to have the book thrown at them, you know. Apparently my great-great-grandmother was sent to Australia. What for depends on who you ask, but either way, she helped build the place. Imagine that! I’d love to have looked up my relatives, seen how the family tree got on over that side of the world. Her daughter, my great-grandmother, stayed behind in Aberdeen. I’ve been there; Aberdeen, that is. It’s an awful place. It’s not the end of the world, but you can see it from there. Thankfully my grandmother married my grandfather, and he was from Edinburgh. Our family has been here ever since.

My grandfather used to say my grandmother stole his heart. She was a wonderful woman. She’d have known what to do to get out of this mess. Survive, somehow. I never met my father and I never really knew my mother. She was present for the first few years and then disappeared under a cloud. I only saw her once more. She turned up on my sixteenth birthday. Wild-eyed and talking ten to the dozen, she was. She wanted me to run away with her and kept ranting on about a curse on the women of this family. I was scared. I’m not one for curses and ghost stories, never have been. Even so, she spooked me. My grandmother stepped in and showed her the door. I’ve often thought how painful that must have been, to have to see off your own daughter like that.

My grandmother put me first. Chose me over her own daughter. Imagine that.

So, anyway, my mother disappeared, popped back in to warn me of a curse, and then I never saw or heard of her again. My grandmother never had much to say about her. The only thing she’d ever say was, "If you dance with the devil long enough, one day he's going to follow you home." I don’t often think about my mother. When I do, she gives me the willies.

You’re getting all the juicy gossip today, aren’t you, constable? I wonder what will come first: your colleagues coming looking for you, or you waking up? I expect you’ll be cross with me when you do. How long do I have, do we think?

Oh dear, you have gone pale.

It’s a shame folk aren’t sent to Australia anymore. I could have crossed it off my wish list, after all these years. More likely Cornton Vale, eh? Or a prison further away? It doesn’t matter if there’s no one to visit me, I suppose. I would miss seeing my George though. Who will tend to his grave? Who will keep him company?

Listen to me. My grandmother will be spinning in her grave, hearing me talk like this... God bless her.

You mustn’t put things off, constable. Live your life, while you can. Imagine waiting all these years to travel, until it’s too late. What am I saying? I’m Scottish, of course I’ve waited. Are you familiar with the Darien Scheme, constab…never mind. That was us trying to strike out on our own. We made a mess of it, of course, and we hitched ourselves to the English to dig ourselves out of that hole. I think we lost our nerve after that and never got it back. In the seventies we voted for devolution. More folks said yes than no, but not enough of us, apparently. Even when we win, we lose. Then there’s the independence referendum. The less said about that, the better. The more I’ve read about our history, the more I feel like we’ve spent over three hundred years afraid of being stung again. I’m eighty-six. I don’t know how many years I’ve got left in me. Maybe I should practise what I preach. Take a dose of courage myself. I’ve waited long enough. Maybe it’s time I grasped the nettle. Or should I say the thistle?

Where was I? I’ve lost my train of thought. Happens a lot when you get to my age, believe me.

I bet your police car goes fast. I haven’t driven in some time, you know. We used to have a blue Ford Fiesta; I expect they’ve changed a bit since then. Where are your car keys then, constable? Let’s have a look, see if we can find them. Ah, here they are! I daresay I won’t make it all the way to Australia, but Scone might be doable. I'm sure my George wouldn't approve… Mind you, he always did say I was stubborn, so he might have expected I’d get there in the end. One way or another.

I'll pop by and see him on the way, tell him all about it. If I leave now, I’ll have time to see him and catch the afternoon tea service. Especially if I can figure out how to put those flashing blue lights on.

Right then, I suppose I’d best be off.

You really were a polite young man. I’m sorry to leave you on your own like this.

I do hope you forgive me when you wake up.

I’m sure you’ll understand.

Take care, dear.

Thank you for reading.
